Graph Theory Basics

Graph theory is a part of math and computer science. It studies graphs – pictures made of dots and lines. The dots, called vertices, can be people, places, or things. The lines, called edges, show how two dots are connected.

Graphs can be undirected (two-way) or directed (one-way). They may also have weights (numbers) to show distance or cost. In simple words, graph theory helps us model real problems like social networks, maps, and computer connections.

More About Graphs

In a graph, a path is a way to go from one dot to another by following lines. A graph is called connected if you can get from any dot to any other. Special graphs include trees (graphs with no loops) and cycles (loops that start and end at the same dot).

Basic Terms

  • Vertex (Node): A dot in the graph.
  • Edge (Link): A line connecting two dots.
  • Path: A route from one dot to another by following edges.
  • Connected Graph: A graph where every dot can reach every other dot.
  • Tree: A connected graph with no loops.
  • Cycle: A loop that starts and ends at the same dot without repeating edges.

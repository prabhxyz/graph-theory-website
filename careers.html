Careers Using Graph Theory

Here are some jobs that rely on graph theory, with a brief look at what they do and typical education paths.

These academics prove new theorems about graphs and teach at universities or research labs. A PhD in mathematics or computer science is common.

Implementing features like route planning, social network tools, or search requires graph algorithms. A bachelor's in CS is typical; advanced roles may need a master's or higher.

Analyzing social networks, transaction graphs, or large-scale data often involves graph metrics and tools (like NetworkX or Neo4j). A background in CS, math, or stats is typical, with strong data analysis skills.

Specializing in Graph Neural Networks or graph-based ML, these roles need strong AI fundamentals (master’s or PhD). They develop new models for recommendations, drug discovery, or fraud detection.

Designing computer networks or telecom systems involves routing protocols and reliability, which rely on graph concepts. A bachelor’s in network engineering or CS plus certifications (e.g., Cisco) is typical.

Solving logistical puzzles (like airline scheduling or supply chain routing) often uses graph-based optimization. A degree in operations research, industrial engineering, or applied math is common.

Studying biological networks (genes, proteins, diseases) often requires graph algorithms. These roles typically have a biology background plus computational skills, often a master’s or PhD.

Mapping and analyzing social networks is crucial in sociology, economics, and marketing. A social science background with data analysis expertise is typical for these roles.

They build or maintain knowledge graphs (e.g. Google Knowledge Graph). A CS background with experience in SPARQL, RDF, or graph databases is essential, often combined with AI or data integration skills.

In insurance and finance, they may use network models to assess how risks interconnect. A math/statistics background plus actuarial exams is typical for actuaries.
